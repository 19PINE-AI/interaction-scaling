\section{Interaction Is a Distinct, Unbounded Axis}
\label{sec:interaction}

\paragraph{Reasoning and sampling saturate; interaction does not (P1).} On $15$ hard code tasks at a fixed $20$K-token budget, we compare four strategies: reasoning-only (extended thinking), best-of-$N$ sampling with an oracle verifier, a single-agent loop (L), and the proposer--reviewer harness (H). Reasoning saturates at $73.3\%$ ($86.7\%$ under a generous thinking-heavy budget partition) and best-of-$N$ at $86.7\%$, while \emph{every} feedback-iterating strategy reaches $\geq 97.8\%$ (\Cref{fig:strategies}). The gap between interaction and the in-token-space axes is $13$--$27$\,pp and is the framework's load-bearing prediction.

\emph{A note on two numerical coincidences} (flagged up front so the tables read cleanly): on this $15$-task suite the default-partition reasoning ceiling and the Phase-1 single-seed single-shot rate are both $73.3\%$ ($11/15$), and the thinking-heavy reasoning partition and the oracle best-of-$N$ ceiling are both $86.7\%$ ($13/15$). These are coincidences of a small suite, not the same measurement; the canonical $3$-seed single-shot rate is $66.7\%$ (\Cref{tab:harness-headline}).

\paragraph{Architecture buys efficiency and reliability.} Among interaction strategies the gap is not in ceiling pass-rate (they tie within seed noise at $N{=}15$) but in cost and variance. The proposer--reviewer harness is the most token-efficient ($1{,}029$ mean output tokens vs.\ $1{,}431$ for the single-agent loop and $1{,}416$ for the oracle-verifier sample-and-select baseline) and the only variant at strict $100\%$ with \emph{zero} seed variance; the single-agent loop hits $100\%$ in $2$ of $3$ seeds. Context isolation (the reviewer sees only what it needs to localize defects), structured critique (a typed defect list, not raw stderr), and role specialization explain the efficiency.

\paragraph{Built-in early stopping.} The loop does not over-revise: on code, across three runs, \emph{every} already-passing single-shot task is submitted at iteration~1 with no revision ($30/30$ instances, zero wasted tokens). The grounded signal---a failing test---is the trigger; without it the loop is silent. This is why code's token overhead is only ${\sim}37\%$ above single-shot despite an iteration cap of $5$.
