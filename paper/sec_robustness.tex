\section{Robustness}
\label{sec:robustness}

\paragraph{Cross-model, code.} The effect is architectural, not something specific to Claude. On the hard code suite, over three seeds per family, the harness lifts all three families we test: Sonnet~4, Qwen3-235B, and GPT-5 (the last from a higher single-shot baseline; \Cref{fig:crossmodel}a). The telling detail is the variance: single-shot fluctuates seed-to-seed for every family, while the reviewed ceiling has \emph{zero} seed variance for all three. The grounded loop converges to the same ceiling regardless of the starting draw.

\paragraph{Cross-model, grounded geometry.} To rule out a Claude- or prompt-specific explanation of the geometry effect, we run the same geometry-feedback harness with \textbf{Gemini~3.1~Pro} as proposer on the dense real-paper slide suite, scored by the identical model-free DOM instrument. The effect reproduces, and is larger (\Cref{fig:crossmodel}b): Gemini's single-shot slides have more defects than Sonnet's, and the grounded loop removes almost all of the excess. Since the instrument uses no model, the effect is neither proposer- nor scorer-specific.

\paragraph{Held-out generalization.} On a $32$-task held-out code suite built after the method was fixed, the harness again recovers every first-shot failure with no regressions and reaches a perfect pass rate (\Cref{tab:heldout}). The absolute lift is smaller only because the held-out single-shot baseline is higher, leaving less headroom.

\paragraph{Budget allocation.} Sweeping how a fixed token budget is split between proposer, execution, and reviewer produces an enormous spread in pass rate (\Cref{fig:allocation}), and it rises monotonically with the proposer's share: review-heavy corners collapse almost to zero, while propose-heavy splits plateau at the top. The best split is \emph{propose-heavy} (give most of the budget to generation, and reserve just enough for the reviewer to locate defects); extra compute is better spent on more samples than on deeper iteration past the early-stop point.
